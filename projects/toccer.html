Tennis Polo (Toccer)

A team field sport I invented in 2004. A tennis and soccer hybrid played ten a side, with a goalkeeper holding a tennis racket. It started by accident at a summer camp and became the most popular thing on the grounds.

How it started

It rained too much in the summer of 2004. I was running the tennis program at Camp Awosting for Boys in Bantam, Connecticut, and the courts kept washing out. Toccer was born out of necessity. We took a tennis-sized ball onto the soccer field and made up a game on the spot.

It exploded. Some days I had a handful of kids who wanted a real tennis lesson and 30+ others streaming onto the soccer field to play. The soccer field director was not thrilled about the encroachment on his domain. Decades later, our rivalry (jokingly) lives on.

By the end of the summer the campers were convinced the game had legs. I bought a domain, and on a rainy day, we wrote some rules.

How it plays

The premise is simple. Two teams, a tennis-sized ball, and a goalkeeper holding a tennis racket. You move the ball by throwing it, kicking it, or running with it, with a three-step limit when you carry it. You score on the goal the keeper defends. Most points wins.

How it spread

In 2010 I brought Toccer to Windridge in Vermont, where I was Head Counselor. The director was wary about kids swinging rackets around, so we agreed rackets would be for goalkeepers only. We ran a one-time pilot, it went so well that it became permanent programming.

That summer was the high-water mark. Toccer joined the camp's color war Olympics, we played around 100 games, and girls got to play for the first time. By the end, counselors were running games on their own. That is as close as I will get to James Naismith watching people play the game he invented.

It traveled to other places too. Several of my high school teams played it. I built an indoor winter version with a foam ball. Every so often I still get an email from a gym teacher who wants to adapt it for their class.

What it is

Toccer grew the way the best camp traditions do, through word of mouth, in communities with the right mix of salesmanship, newness, and boredom. That is the elixir. Pickleball, cornhole, and ultimate frisbee found their footing because people already knew the shape of them. Toccer found its footing inside the places that played it.

Twenty years on, it bears my resemblance: iteration, creativity, and brazen confidence. It has a rulebook, a Wikipedia entry, and a small map of places where people have swung a racket in goal. Staging the first collegiate Tennis Polo match might be fun someday.

I invented a sport by accident, wrote it down, and watched other people make it theirs. That was always the point.
