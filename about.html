Ron Bronson

Finds messy systems. Makes them legible. Builds what’s missing. Keeps them moving.

The practice

For about twenty years I’ve worked on how technology reshapes civic life — and how public systems stay trustworthy as AI and automation move into consequential decisions. My work sits where strategy becomes structure: clarifying ambiguous goals, organizing teams and systems, and keeping trust embedded in the technology people are required to depend on.

It is not aesthetic-first or corporate-gloss work. It is infrastructure, public-interest systems, and the unglamorous plumbing that decides whether something can actually be trusted.

How I work now

Today most of that practice runs through agents. I direct AI agents to design and ship complex systems — orchestrating the work, keeping a human in the loop where judgment matters, and writing the decisions down so the system stays legible long after it ships.

The result is whole systems, not mockups: deterministic engines, analytics layers, governance tooling — built end to end and documented in decision records rather than slideware. The catalog is the evidence.

Where it comes from

This isn’t a pivot. It’s service design, consequence design, and agent experience (AX) applied to a new substrate. The questions are the ones I’ve always asked — who bears the consequences, where does the system break, what’s actually missing — now answered at the speed and scale that directing agents allows.
